TARGETING AGING

Senescent cell removal, insulin signaling inhibition/caloric restriction, and rapamycin extend lifespan in mice the most in our meta-analysis of over 100 different aging interventions. Surprisingly, the natural molecule fisetin can do all three, clearing senescent cells and acting as a dual inhibitor in both insulin and mTOR signaling, offerening great promise as a therapeutic for aging-related diseases. However, fisetin has low bioavailability and is quickly cleared from the body, limiting its effectiveness.

Due to its excellent safety profile, clinical trials using large doses of fisetin are currently underway in hopes it can help in human aging despite these drawbacks. At ETTA, we are trying a better way, making formulations of fisetin that can deliver it to treat a wide range of diseases that occur during aging.

As fisetin has been shown to treat multiple skin conditions in mice, including psoriasis, dermatitis, UV damage, and melanoma, we will first use our fisetin formulations to target the skin. The accelerated aging diseases Xeroderma Pigmentosum and Cockayne Syndrome present skin conditions in patients, and fisetin has been shown to treat the mouse model of these two diseases. We hope to treat these patients as a proof-of-principle that we can be the first to treat diseases of aging in humans.
